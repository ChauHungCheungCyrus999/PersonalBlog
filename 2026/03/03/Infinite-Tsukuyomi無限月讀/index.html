Is Infinite Tsukuyomi really a bad thing?

What is the Infinite Tsukuyomi?

In short: the Infinite Tsukuyomi is a fictional genjutsu from Naruto. It’s a world-scale illusion cast through the moon (via the Rinne Sharingan) that traps everyone in a dreamlike, idealized reality while their bodies are immobilized in the real world.

A personal note

I first encountered the Infinite Tsukuyomi around 2022 — during one of the darkest periods of my life. That experience made me wonder: why would Naruto want to stop it? If everyone could live in a perfect dream, reunite with lost loved ones, and never suffer again, wouldn’t that be paradise? No more wars, no more crime, no more visible pain — it sounds appealing at first glance.

Why it can feel like paradise

People online argue the Tsukuyomi lets you live out anything you want. In the dream, worries disappear; desires become reality. Want something to happen? It happens. From that perspective, it looks like the ultimate cure for sorrow: a guaranteed, uninterrupted experience of whatever makes you happiest.

Why it’s deeply troubling

There’s a darker side. Even if the dream feels perfect, it’s forced, not chosen, and that matters. It takes away people’s freedom — they can’t decide for themselves, so any happiness starts to feel like being trapped. Things in the dream aren’t real: achievements, friendships, and personal growth become empty. While minds float in fantasy, bodies and communities can be harmed or ignored. The power to control everyone is too dangerous; if it’s abused or breaks, the result could be awful. And without hardship, we stop growing and creating, so life becomes flat. Taken together, these problems show a forced paradise can be worse than an imperfect real life.

My take — a drug that never quits

After thinking and rethinking my experiences, the Infinite Tsukuyomi feels like an extreme, undying drug. Imagine something between an anesthetic and a euphoric stimulant: it pumps dopamine endlessly, promising continuous bliss until the body and mind are drained. But like any addiction, the price shows later — memory loss, loss of abilities, and the surrender of control.

The core choice

So the question becomes simple and sharp: would you trade your freedom and control for a stretch of perfect happiness, or keep control of your life even though it includes both suffering and joy? Is a brief, enforced paradise worth losing who you are?
